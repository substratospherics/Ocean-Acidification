Ocean Acidification and Organisms

What is ocean acidification?

An increase in the acidity of the ocean is known as ocean acidification. This can have huge impacts on marine organisms as the concentration of carbonic acid in seawater gets higher and the pH decreases, making the water more acidic. This change in pH can alter the physiology, behavior, and ecology of many marine organisms in several ways, which we will cover on this website.

1. Calcifying Organisms

Calcium carbonate (CaCO3) is used by oceanic calcifying organisms like corals, mollusks, and some planktonic species to construct and preserve their exoskeletons. When calcium ions (Ca2+) and carbonate ions (CO32-) combine in seawater, a mineral called calcium carbonate is created.

Many chemical processes take place throughout the calcification process. In order to create calcium carbonate, calcium ions and carbonate ions in seawater must first interact. The concentration of calcium and carbonate ions in the seawater, which is regulated by elements including temperature, salinity, and CO2 levels, is a prerequisite for this reaction.

However, more carbon dioxide (CO2) molecules mix with water molecules to create carbonic acid (H2CO3) as the amount of CO2 in the ocean rises. The concentration of carbonate ions in seawater decreases as a result of the increase in acidity, making it more challenging for calcifying organisms to produce their calcium carbonate structures.

The dissociation of calcium carbonate into its component ions as a result of the pH drop further limits the amount of carbonate ions available to calcifying organisms. Dissolution is the process involved, and it has the potential to weaken and erode existing calcium carbonate formations in the ocean.

In general, a variety of environmental conditions, such as temperature, salinity, and the concentration of CO2 in the water, have an impact on the chemistry of calcification. The chemistry of calcification is disturbed as ocean CO2 concentrations rise, making it harder for calcifying species to construct and maintain their structures, which can have a big influence on marine ecosystems.

2. Non-calcifying Organisms

Fish and some types of algae in the water are non-calcifying creatures and do not require calcium carbonate for structural support, but they are nevertheless sensitive to pH fluctuations. As the amount of CO2 in the ocean rises, carbonic acid is created, which lowers the pH of the seawater and increases its acidity. The chemistry of the seawater is impacted by this pH variation, which may have an effect on the physiology and behavior of marine species.

The ion balance in the cells of marine species can change when the acidity rises. The hydrogen ions (H+) from carbonic acid, in particular, have the ability to impair the normal operation of proteins, enzymes, and other cellular functions. This may result in a slowed metabolism, slower growth, and a compromised immune system.

Additionally, the intake of nutrients by marine species may be impacted by the acidity of seawater. For instance, the growth and reproduction of some species of algae and plankton depend on the presence of dissolved nutrients like nitrate and phosphate. The availability of essential nutrients may decrease with an increase in acidity, making it more challenging for these organisms to survive.

The behavior of marine species can also be impacted by the acidity of saltwater. According to certain research, certain fish species' sense of smell can be affected by an increase in acidity, making it harder for them to find food and avoid predators.

In the end, the rise in ocean CO2 and the resulting acidification can have a variety of effects on the physiology, behavior, and ecology of non-calcifying organisms. Depending on the species and the particular environmental circumstances, the precise effects may vary, but the overall tendency is toward harming marine ecosystems.

3. Effects on the Food Web

Ocean acidification's effects on species distribution, abundance, and interspecies interactions are referred to as "food web effects" in the ocean. Ocean acidification-related modifications to the physiology and behavior of calcifying and non-calcifying organisms may indirectly affect the food web.

For instance, the disappearance of calcifying organisms, such as corals and mollusks, may result in less food being available for other species that depend on them. This can include tiny herbivores that nibble on the algae that develops on the shell's surface or larger predators that eat the organisms directly.

In addition, the ocean's nutrition cycle may be impacted by the decline in calcifying organisms. Calcium carbonate shells give bacteria a surface on which to develop and create biofilms, which are crucial for the cycling of nutrients. The availability of these biofilms and the nutrients they hold may decline when these shells dissolve as a result of ocean acidification, affecting the entire food web.

Also, the range and number of non-calcifying organisms can be impacted by ocean acidification. For instance, several studies have demonstrated that a rise in acidity can alter the behavior of some fish species, resulting in decreased activity levels and a decreased capacity to locate prey or evade predators.The loss of important predator or prey species can have a ripple effect on the environment, which can affect the entire food web.

As a whole, it might be challenging to forecast how ocean acidification will affect the food chain because these effects are complicated. However, it is evident that modifications in the ocean's chemistry brought on by an increase in CO2 can have major indirect effects on species distribution, abundance, and interactions, ultimately affecting the entire marine ecosystem.
